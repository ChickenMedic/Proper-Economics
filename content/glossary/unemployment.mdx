short: Wanting a job and actively looking, but not finding one.

The unemployment rate isn't "people without jobs." Retirees, students, and stay-at-home parents don't count. To be unemployed, in the official sense, you have to want a job and be actively looking — and still not have one. The rate is the share of those willing workers who can't find work.

Economists slice it into flavors, because the cure depends on the cause. *Frictional* unemployment is people between jobs — normal, even healthy, in a changing economy. *Structural* unemployment is a mismatch: the mill closed, and the town's skills no longer fit the jobs on offer. *Cyclical* unemployment is the painful kind that surges in recessions, when there simply isn't enough spending to keep everyone employed.

Because of the frictional churn, zero unemployment isn't the goal — an economy where nobody ever switched jobs would be frozen, not healthy. The real debates are about the cyclical kind: how much government or central-bank action can cure it, and at what price in inflation. That argument runs straight through Keynes, Friedman, and the stagflation of the 1970s.

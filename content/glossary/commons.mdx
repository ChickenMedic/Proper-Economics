---
term: The commons
slug: commons
short: A shared resource anyone can use — and everyone can overuse.
related: [public-good, externality]
---

Picture a village pasture where anyone may graze cattle. Each herder reasons: one more cow benefits *me* fully, while the extra grazing cost is split among everyone. So everyone adds a cow, then another — and the pasture is eaten to mud. Each choice was individually sensible; the sum was collective ruin.

That's the "tragedy of the commons," a phrase made famous by ecologist Garrett Hardin in 1968. A commons is any shared resource that's open to all and used up by use: fisheries, groundwater, the atmosphere. Nobody owns it, so nobody has an incentive to conserve it — overfished oceans and climate change are the pasture problem at planetary scale. The textbook fixes were two: privatize it (an owner protects what's hers) or regulate it (the state sets limits).

Then Elinor Ostrom went and checked. Studying real commons — Swiss alpine pastures, Spanish irrigation systems, Maine lobster fisheries — she found communities managing shared resources successfully for centuries with their own rules, monitoring, and penalties: neither market nor state. The "tragedy," it turns out, is a risk, not a fate. That work made her the first woman to win the economics Nobel, in 2009.

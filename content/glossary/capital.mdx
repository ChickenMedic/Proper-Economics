short: Wealth used to produce more wealth - machines, buildings, tools, funds.

A fisherman who spends a week weaving a net catches nothing that week - but far more fish every week after. The net is capital: wealth that isn't consumed today, but used to produce more wealth tomorrow.

That's the core meaning: machines, buildings, tools, and the money that buys them. Capital is one of the classic ingredients of production, alongside land and labor, and building more of it is a big part of how poor economies become rich ones. Note the catch in the fisherman's story, though - capital always starts as sacrifice. Someone had to skip a week of fishing (or lend him the fish to get by) before the net existed. That waiting is part of why capital earns a return.

The word has since colonized the language: *human* capital (your skills and education), *social* capital (your network of trust), even *political* capital. And for Marx, "capital" wasn't just stuff but a social relationship - which is why he named the book after it. When you meet the word, it's worth checking which meaning is on duty.

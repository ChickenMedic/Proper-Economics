---
term: Money supply
slug: money-supply
short: The total amount of money circulating in an economy.
related: [inflation, monetary-policy, quantity-theory-of-money]
---

How much money exists? Not just the bills in wallets — also the balance in your checking account, which is money you can spend even though no paper version of it may exist anywhere. Add up all the money in an economy — cash plus bank deposits, in widening circles economists label M1, M2, and so on — and you have the money supply.

Here's the part that surprises people: most money isn't printed, it's *lent into existence*. When a bank issues a loan, it credits the borrower's account with new spendable money. So the money supply isn't a fixed pile of coins; it swells and shrinks with lending, and the central bank steers it — mainly through interest rates — rather than controlling it dial-by-dial.

Why watch it? Because the amount of money sloshing around is tied, loosely in the short run and tightly in the long run, to inflation. Milton Friedman built monetarism on that link, and central banks have argued ever since about how closely to watch the money numbers versus interest rates. Either way: when the money supply grows far faster than the economy for long, prices eventually follow.

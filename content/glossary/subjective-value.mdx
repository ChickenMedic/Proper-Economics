---
term: Subjective value
slug: subjective-value
short: The idea that value comes from what people want, not what things cost to make.
related: [marginal-utility, labor-theory-of-value]
---

An umbrella is worth more to you in a downpour than on a sunny day - same umbrella, same factory, same labor. A vintage concert T-shirt is junk to one person and priceless to the fan who was there. Value, it seems, isn't stored *in* objects at all. It lives in the eye of the beholder.

That's the theory of subjective value: things are valuable because people want them, not because of what they cost to produce. It sounds obvious, but it was a bombshell. Classical economists from Smith to Marx had tied value to the labor that went into things. In the 1870s, Carl Menger and others flipped the logic: no amount of honest work makes something valuable if nobody wants it, and desire alone can make a scribbled autograph worth a fortune.

The flip had consequences. It dissolved old puzzles (why water is cheap and diamonds dear), knocked a leg out from under Marx's system, and explains why every voluntary trade can leave *both* sides better off - each walks away with what they valued more. Not bad for an idea your umbrella already knew.

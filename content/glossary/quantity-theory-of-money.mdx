---
term: Quantity theory of money
slug: quantity-theory-of-money
short: The idea that printing much more money mainly raises prices.
related: [money-supply, inflation, monetary-policy]
---

Suppose everyone in the country woke up with twice as much money in their account. Feels like Christmas — until everyone goes shopping. There are no extra goods on the shelves, just extra money chasing them, so sellers raise prices, and before long your doubled money buys about what your old money did.

That thought experiment is the heart of the quantity theory of money: over the long run, the amount of money in circulation determines the level of prices, and printing a lot more of it mainly buys you inflation. The idea is old — thinkers noticed prices jumping when Spanish galleons unloaded American silver in the 1500s — and Irving Fisher gave it its famous equation in the 1900s.

Milton Friedman revived the theory in the mid-twentieth century, summing it up in one line: inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon. The fine print matters — the link is loose in the short run, and money can shift around in speed as well as amount — but every hyperinflation in history, from Weimar Germany to Zimbabwe, has featured a printing press running hot.

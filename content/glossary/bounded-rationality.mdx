---
term: Bounded rationality
slug: bounded-rationality
short: People make good-enough choices, not perfect ones — thinking is costly.
related: [nudge, rational-expectations]
---

The textbook way to buy a used car: gather every listing, weigh depreciation curves, calculate expected repair costs, and pick the mathematically optimal vehicle. The human way: check a few listings, ask your brother-in-law, and buy the blue one that seemed fine. You didn't optimize. You found something good enough and got on with your life.

Herbert Simon called this bounded rationality: people are rational, but only within limits — limited time, limited information, limited brainpower. Instead of *optimizing*, we "satisfice" (his blend of satisfy and suffice): search until something clears the good-enough bar, then stop. Given that thinking itself is costly, that shortcut isn't a failure of reason — it's often the reasonable move.

The idea was a quiet rebellion against economics' beloved perfectly rational calculator-person, and it opened the door for Kahneman, Tversky, and Thaler to map exactly *how* real decisions bend — the mental shortcuts, the biases, the autopilot. Modern behavioral economics, and every nudge in your HR paperwork, stands on Simon's unglamorous observation: nobody has time to be a textbook.

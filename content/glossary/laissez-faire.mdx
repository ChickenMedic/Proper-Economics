---
term: Laissez-faire
slug: laissez-faire
short: The view that government should mostly leave markets alone.
related: [invisible-hand, free-trade, market-economy]
---

The story goes that a French finance minister asked a group of merchants what the government could do to help them. Their reply: *laissez-nous faire* — roughly, "leave us alone." The phrase stuck, and "laissez-faire" became the banner for a whole outlook: the economy works best when the government mostly stays out of the way.

The idea took shape with the French physiocrats in the 1700s and got its intellectual muscle from Adam Smith, who argued that competition and self-interest coordinate an economy better than ministers and decrees can. In the laissez-faire view, the state should protect property, enforce contracts, keep the peace — and otherwise let prices, not politicians, decide what gets made.

Almost nobody, including Smith, has held the pure version; he wanted public roads, schools, and limits on banks. The real argument, running from Smith through Keynes and Hayek to today, is about where to draw the line — and every generation redraws it.

---
term: Flat tax
slug: flat-tax
short: One single tax rate for everyone, regardless of income.
related: [progressive-tax, vat]
---

One rate, everyone, done. A flat tax charges the same percentage of income whether you earn $30,000 or $3 million — say 15% across the board. The millionaire still pays far more in dollars; what's flat is the *rate*.

The pitch is simplicity and incentives. No bracket games, fewer loopholes to lobby for, and — supporters argue — no penalty on earning more, since your next dollar is taxed like your first. Estonia became the poster child after adopting a flat income tax in 1994, and a wave of Eastern European countries followed. Most real versions exempt the first chunk of income, which quietly makes them mildly progressive anyway.

The case against is about who carries the load. A single rate takes a much bigger bite out of a tight budget than a comfortable one — 15% of a paycheck that barely covers rent hurts more than 15% of a fortune (diminishing marginal utility, again). Critics also note the simplicity argument slightly cheats: what makes tax codes gnarly is mostly defining *income*, not looking up the rate. The flat-versus-progressive fight is really a values question wearing math.

---
term: Aggregate demand
slug: aggregate-demand
short: Total spending in the whole economy by households, firms, and government.
related: [gdp, fiscal-policy, recession]
---

Picture every purchase in the country happening at once: your groceries, a family's new roof, a company's new delivery vans, the government's road repairs, a foreign buyer ordering machine parts. Add it all up and you have aggregate demand - the total spending in an economy.

Why give it a name? Because John Maynard Keynes built a revolution on it. His point was that one person's spending is another person's income. If everyone gets nervous and cuts back at once, businesses see empty tills, lay people off, and the laid-off spend even less. The economy can get stuck in a slump not because it *can't* produce, but because nobody's buying - factories idle, workers willing, spending missing.

That idea flipped the policy world. If slumps come from too little total spending, then someone - usually the government or central bank - can lean against the wind and spend, or make spending cheaper, when everyone else won't. How well that works, and at what cost, is still the liveliest argument in macroeconomics.

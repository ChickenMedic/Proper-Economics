---
term: Comparative advantage
slug: comparative-advantage
short: Do what costs you least to do, and trade for the rest.
related: [free-trade, opportunity-cost, division-of-labor]
---

Suppose a star lawyer also happens to type faster than her assistant. Should she do her own typing? No — every hour she spends typing is an hour not spent on legal work that pays fifty times more. She's better at both jobs, but she's *much* better at one, so she should stick to law and let the assistant type.

That's comparative advantage, David Ricardo's great insight about trade. What matters isn't being the best at something; it's what you give up to do it. Everyone — every person, every country — gives up less doing some things than others, so everyone has something worth specializing in.

The startling conclusion: two countries can both gain from trade even if one of them is worse at making everything. Each focuses on what costs it least and trades for the rest, and both end up with more. Economists rank it among the least obvious true ideas in the field — which may explain why trade debates never quite die.

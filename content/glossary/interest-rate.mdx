---
term: Interest rate
slug: interest-rate
short: The price of borrowing money, usually a percentage per year.
related: [monetary-policy, capital, money-supply]
---

Borrow $10,000 for a car at 5% interest and you'll pay back the $10,000 plus about $500 for the first year. That extra $500 is the price of not waiting - of getting the car now with someone else's money instead of saving up for it.

An interest rate is exactly that: the price of borrowing, expressed as a percentage per year. Like any price, it balances two sides - savers, who must be paid to part with their money for a while, and borrowers, who'll pay for the head start. It also folds in the lender's risks: that you won't pay it back, and that inflation will shrink the dollars you return. Riskier borrower, higher rate.

Interest rates are also the main lever of monetary policy. When a central bank raises its benchmark rate, borrowing costs ripple through the economy - mortgages, business loans, credit cards - and spending cools; cutting rates does the reverse. That's why a quarter-point decision by a committee in Washington or Frankfurt can change your monthly payment, and why the whole financial world hangs on their every word.

---
term: Fiscal policy
slug: fiscal-policy
short: Government using its taxing and spending to speed up or slow the economy.
related: [monetary-policy, aggregate-demand, recession]
---

Remember the checks governments mailed out during the pandemic? Or a new highway project that appears just as the local factory closes? That's fiscal policy: the government using its own budget - taxes and spending - to speed up or slow down the economy.

The logic, owed largely to John Maynard Keynes, goes like this. In a slump, everyone cuts back at once: households save, businesses stop investing, and the economy shrinks for lack of spending. The government is the one player big enough to lean the other way - spend more, tax less, and put money in motion when no one else will. In a boom, ideally, it does the reverse.

That "ideally" is where the arguments live. Critics point out that stimulus is politically easy and restraint is politically painful, so debts tend to pile up; others, following economists like Milton Friedman and James Buchanan, doubt governments can time it right at all. Fiscal policy's sibling is monetary policy - same goal, different lever, run by the central bank instead.

---
term: Recession
slug: recession
short: A months-long slump when the whole economy shrinks and jobs get scarce.
related: [depression, business-cycle, unemployment]

You can often feel a recession before anyone officially names it. The "we're hiring" signs come down. A friend gets laid off, then another. Restaurants get quieter, and companies announce they're "pausing" things. The economy, which normally grows a little every year, has shifted into reverse.

A recession is a broad, months-long decline in economic activity - output falling, unemployment rising, spending pulling back, all at once. A common shorthand is two straight quarters of shrinking GDP, though in the U.S. the official call is made (often long after the fact) by a committee of economists at the National Bureau of Economic Research, who weigh jobs, income, and output together.

Recessions are the down phase of the business cycle, and explaining them is one of macroeconomics' founding jobs. Keynes blamed collapses in total spending that feed on themselves - your canceled purchase is someone else's lost income. Others point to bursting bubbles, oil shocks, or central banks hitting the brakes too hard. Most recessions end within a year or so; the rare ones that spiral much deeper get a scarier name.

---
term: Transaction costs
slug: transaction-costs
short: The time, hassle, and expense of making a deal happen at all.
related: [externality, market-economy]
---

Why don't you hire a different freelance cook for every dinner? The cooking might even be better - but imagine the searching, interviewing, haggling, and scheduling, every single night. The meal is cheap; the *deal-making* is exhausting. So instead you have a kitchen, or a household, or a favorite takeout place on speed dial.

All that friction - finding a trading partner, negotiating terms, writing contracts, making sure people follow through - is transaction costs: the cost of using the market itself. Ronald Coase used the idea to answer a question nobody had thought to ask: if markets are so good, why do companies exist? Why do firms hire employees and run departments instead of buying everything through one-off deals? Because inside a firm, you skip the daily haggling. Firms grow until organizing one more thing internally costs more than just buying it - that insight helped win Coase a Nobel Prize.

Once you see transaction costs, they're everywhere: they're why eBay and Uber were revolutions (they crushed the cost of finding and trusting strangers), why standard contracts exist, and why some sensible-sounding deals just never happen. Markets aren't free to use - and much of economic life is shaped by the fees.

---
term: Means of production
slug: means-of-production
short: The factories, land, tools, and machines used to make things.
related: [capital, socialism, surplus-value]
---

A baker needs an oven, a farmer needs land, a coder needs servers. The stuff you need to *make* things — factories, land, machines, tools — is what Karl Marx called the means of production. Not the things themselves, but the things that make the things.

Why give equipment such a grand name? Because for Marx, who owns it decides everything. Own the means of production and you can hire others and keep the profit from what they make. Own nothing but your hands and you must work for whoever does. That single dividing line — owners versus workers, capital versus labor — was, in his telling, the engine of history and the root of class conflict.

It's also the crispest way to define the twentieth century's great economic contest. Socialism, in its classic sense, means the means of production are owned collectively — by the state or by communities — rather than privately. Capitalism means they're owned privately and run for profit. Most real economies mix the two; the argument is over the proportions.

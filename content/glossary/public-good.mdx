---
term: Public good
slug: public-good
short: Something everyone can use without paying or using it up, like streetlights.
related: [externality, commons]
---

A streetlight shines on everyone who walks past. You can't send a bill to each pedestrian, and one person enjoying the light leaves just as much for the next. Same with national defense, clean air, and the tornado siren: once they exist, everybody's covered, payer or not.

Economists call these public goods, and they're defined by two features. Nobody can be excluded from using them, and one person's use doesn't use them up. That combination creates the free-rider problem: if you get the benefit whether or not you chip in, why chip in? Everyone reasons the same way, the money never materializes, and the lighthouse — the textbook favorite — doesn't get built.

This is why even staunchly market-friendly economists, Adam Smith included, carve out a role for government here: some things markets genuinely won't supply, so taxes do. One caution: "public good" doesn't mean "good for the public" or "anything the government provides." Schools and trains are valuable, but you can charge for a seat — so the term, strictly used, is narrower than the politics around it.

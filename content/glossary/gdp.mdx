term: GDP
slug: gdp
short: The total value of everything a country produces in a year.

Imagine adding up the price of everything your country made this year - every haircut, every app, every truckload of wheat, every hospital visit. That grand total is GDP: gross domestic product, the standard measure of the size of an economy.

The rules matter. Only *final* goods count (the bread, not also the flour that went into it, or you'd count the wheat three times), and only new production counts - selling your used car adds nothing, because the car was counted when it was built. When GDP grows, the country produced more than last year; when it shrinks for a sustained stretch, that's a recession.

GDP is genuinely useful and genuinely incomplete, and economists have said so from the start - Simon Kuznets, who built the modern accounts in the 1930s, warned against reading it as welfare. It skips housework and volunteering, ignores whether growth came from hospitals or cigarettes, and says nothing about who got the money. It measures the size of the pie, not how it tastes or how it's sliced.

---
term: Marginal
slug: marginal
short: Referring to the effect of one more unit — the next one, not the average.
related: [marginal-utility, opportunity-cost]
---

You've had two slices of pizza and you're eyeing a third. The question your stomach is actually asking isn't "is pizza good?" — it's "is *one more slice* good?" That's a different question, and the answer changes as you go. Slice one: bliss. Slice three: maybe. Slice six: regret.

"Marginal" is the economist's word for that next-unit thinking. The marginal cost is what one more unit costs to make; the marginal benefit is what one more unit is worth to you. Decisions, it turns out, happen at the margin. Nobody chooses between "all pizza" and "no pizza" — you choose whether to have another slice.

This one word powered a revolution in the 1870s, when economists like Jevons, Menger, and Walras rebuilt the theory of value around it. Once you start noticing margins — one more employee, one more mile, one more episode at 1 a.m. — you can't stop.

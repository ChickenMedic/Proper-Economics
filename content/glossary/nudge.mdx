---
term: Nudge
slug: nudge
short: Steering choices by changing how options are presented, not what's allowed.
related: [bounded-rationality, incentive]
---

When companies switched retirement savings from "sign up if you want in" to "you're in unless you opt out," participation jumped dramatically — in some studies from around half of workers to over 90%. Nothing was banned. Nothing was subsidized. The options were identical; only the default changed.

That's a nudge: steering people's choices by changing how options are presented, without forbidding anything or changing prices. The term comes from Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein, who built on a key finding of behavioral economics — that people are predictably imperfect deciders. We procrastinate, stick with defaults, and follow the crowd. Since *some* default has to exist anyway, they argued, why not pick the one that helps? Fruit at eye level in the cafeteria; organ donation as the default; tax letters that mention most neighbors pay on time.

Nudges have their critics. Some find "we know what's good for you" paternalism creepy, however gentle; others note effects are often modest and some flagship results shrank under re-testing — a contested corner of the field. Still, dozens of governments now run "nudge units," making this one of the few economic ideas you can find hiding in your HR paperwork.

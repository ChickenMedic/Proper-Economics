In the early 1930s, about one in four American workers had no job. Banks failed by the thousands, taking families' savings with them. U.S. output fell by roughly a third, and breadlines wrapped around city blocks — for years. That was the Great Depression, and it's why the word still carries a chill.

A depression is a recession's monstrous cousin: deeper, longer, and more destructive. There's no official cutoff — the old joke says a recession is when your neighbor loses his job, a depression is when you lose yours — but the hallmarks are unemployment in the double digits, falling prices (deflation) that make debts crush harder, waves of bank failures, and a slump measured in years rather than months.

The Great Depression rewired economics. It made Keynes's case that economies can stay stuck without help, launched the New Deal, and set the terms of every macro debate since — Friedman and Schwartz later argued the Federal Reserve turned a bad recession into a catastrophe by letting the money supply collapse. When policymakers flooded the system with support in 2008 and 2020, they were, above all, trying not to repeat the 1930s.

term: VAT
slug: vat
short: A consumption tax collected in stages, built into the shelf price.

If you've shopped in Europe, you've paid VAT without seeing it: unlike an American sales tax added at the register, value-added tax is already baked into the shelf price. A fifth or so of what you paid for that souvenir went to the government.

The clever part is *how* it's collected: in stages, not all at once. The flour mill pays tax on the value it adds to wheat, the bakery on the value it adds to flour, the shop on its markup — with each business deducting the tax already paid upstream. Economically it works out like a tax on final consumption, but because every firm's deduction depends on the previous firm's receipts, the chain largely polices itself. Tax collectors love it for a reason.

Invented in its modern form in France in 1954, VAT has since spread to most of the world's countries — the United States being the famous holdout. The standing objection: since poorer households spend more of their income rather than saving it, a consumption tax bites them proportionally harder. Many countries soften this with reduced rates on food and essentials, at the price of the simplicity that made VAT attractive in the first place.

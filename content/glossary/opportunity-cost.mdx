---
term: Opportunity cost
slug: opportunity-cost
short: The value of the best thing you gave up by choosing this one.
related: [comparative-advantage, marginal, incentive]
---

A "free" Saturday afternoon concert isn't free. It costs you the hike you'd have taken, the nap you needed, or the shift you could have picked up. The ticket price is zero; the cost is whatever you gave up to be there.

That's opportunity cost: the value of the best alternative you passed up. It's arguably the most useful single idea in economics, because it changes what "cost" means. The cost of college isn't just tuition — it's also four years of paychecks you didn't earn. The cost of a city building a stadium isn't just the construction bill — it's the hospitals or tax cuts that money can no longer fund.

Economists get twitchy about this because the forgone option is invisible. Frédéric Bastiat called it "the unseen": everyone admires the stadium; no one can point to the clinic that was never built. Train yourself to ask "compared to what?" and "what's this *really* costing me?" and you're doing economics, whether you meant to or not.

---
term: Rational expectations
slug: rational-expectations
short: The theory that people anticipate policy, so it can't fool them for long.
related: [bounded-rationality, monetary-policy, inflation]
---

Announce a sale for next Friday and nobody shops on Thursday. People don't just react to what happens — they act on what they *expect* to happen. Now apply that to a government: if everyone knows the central bank pumps up the economy before elections, workers and businesses will raise wages and prices in advance, canceling the boost before it lands.

That's the core of rational expectations, the idea Robert Lucas rode to a Nobel Prize: people form their forecasts using all available information — including their understanding of what policymakers will do — so they can't be systematically fooled. They'll be wrong plenty, but not wrong the same way over and over. The sting was aimed at 1970s-era policy tinkering: if a stimulus only works when it surprises people, it stops working once it becomes routine.

The theory's assumption — that ordinary people forecast like sharp statisticians — has taken fire from behavioral economists, who document just how un-statistical we are (see bounded rationality). But its practical lesson stuck and reshaped central banking: credibility is the whole game. It's why central bankers guard their inflation targets like crown jewels and weigh every syllable — the audience is listening, and pricing it in.

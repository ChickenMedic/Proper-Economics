---
term: Stagflation
slug: stagflation
short: The miserable combo of rising prices and a stagnant, jobless economy.
related: [inflation, unemployment, monetary-policy]
---

Imagine prices at the store climbing month after month while, at the same time, the factory in town is laying people off. Money buys less *and* jobs are scarce. The 1970s served exactly that dish across the Western world: in the U.S., inflation and unemployment both pushed toward double digits while drivers queued around the block for gasoline.

The ugly word for that ugly combination is stagflation - stagnation plus inflation. It wasn't supposed to be possible. Postwar Keynesian thinking treated inflation and unemployment as a trade-off, a dial you could turn: accept a bit more of one, get a bit less of the other. Stagflation broke the dial. Both went up together, and the reigning theory had no good answer.

The episode remade macroeconomics. Milton Friedman, who had predicted the trade-off would collapse once people came to *expect* inflation, was vindicated, and central banks absorbed the lesson - painfully, via the Fed's brutal rate hikes around 1980 - that keeping inflation expectations anchored comes first. Every time an oil shock hits, you'll hear the word again, along with a collective shudder from economists of a certain age.

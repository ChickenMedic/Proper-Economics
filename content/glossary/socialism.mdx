Few words in economics get thrown around more loosely. Strictly speaking, socialism is an economic system in which the major means of production - factories, land, big industry - are owned collectively (usually by the state, sometimes by cooperatives) rather than by private individuals, with production directed toward shared goals instead of private profit.

The idea predates Marx - early "utopian socialists" like Robert Owen built model factory towns and cooperative communities - but Marx and Engels gave it its most influential form, arguing that capitalism's own conflicts would push society toward collective ownership. In the twentieth century, the Soviet Union and its imitators tried the full version: state ownership plus central planning of what gets produced. The economic record - chronic shortages, queues, and the calculation problems Mises and Hayek predicted - is a major thread of this site.

One distinction worth keeping crisp: Nordic countries like Sweden and Denmark are often called socialist, but their industries are overwhelmingly privately owned. They're market economies with high taxes and generous welfare states - a different animal from collective ownership, whatever the campaign ads say.

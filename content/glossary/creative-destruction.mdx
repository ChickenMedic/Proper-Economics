---
term: Creative destruction
slug: creative-destruction
short: New products and firms wiping out old ones - how markets renew themselves.
related: [business-cycle, market-economy, incentive]
---

Blockbuster Video had thousands of stores, then streaming arrived and it had roughly one (it's in Oregon; people take selfies there). The smartphone in your pocket did the same to cameras, maps, alarm clocks, and answering machines. Something new was created; something old was destroyed. Repeat forever.

Joseph Schumpeter called this creative destruction, and he thought it - not price competition over pennies - was the real engine of capitalism. The competition that matters isn't the corner store undercutting its rival; it's the innovation that makes the whole category obsolete. In his telling, the market economy is a "perennial gale," constantly tearing down its own structures to build better ones, with entrepreneurs as the storm front.

The gains are enormous but the destruction is not a metaphor: real workers, firms, and towns absorb it, and the new jobs rarely land where the old ones died. Schumpeter's unsettling twist was to predict that capitalism's very success would breed comfortable resistance to the gale. Every debate about disrupted industries - from automation to AI - is arguing about how much wind to allow.

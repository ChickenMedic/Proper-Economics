When the news says "the Fed raised rates" and suddenly mortgages, car loans, and credit cards all cost more, you've just watched monetary policy in action. Somewhere, a committee of central bankers decided the economy needed cooling down — and your monthly payment is how the message reached you.

Monetary policy is how a central bank (the Federal Reserve in the U.S., the ECB in Europe, the Bank of England in the U.K.) steers the economy by adjusting interest rates and the supply of money. Economy overheating, prices climbing too fast? Raise rates: borrowing gets expensive, spending slows, inflation cools. Economy slumping? Cut rates: borrowing gets cheap, spending and hiring pick up.

It's a blunt instrument with a long delay — economists compare it to steering a ship, since today's rate change takes months to fully land. Its sibling is fiscal policy, where governments steer with taxes and spending instead. Much of twentieth-century economics was an argument about which lever to trust.

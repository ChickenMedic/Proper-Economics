---
term: Deflation
slug: deflation
short: Prices falling across the whole economy — less pleasant than it sounds.
related: [inflation, depression, monetary-policy]
---

Falling prices sound like a permanent storewide sale. What's not to like? Here's the catch: if you expect everything to be cheaper next month, why buy now? So you wait. Millions of people wait. Stores sell less, cut wages or jobs, people spend even less — and prices fall further.

That downward spiral is deflation: a sustained drop in prices across the whole economy. It also plays a nasty trick on anyone with debt. Your mortgage payment stays fixed while wages and prices fall, so the debt gets *heavier* in real terms. Irving Fisher called this "debt-deflation" and blamed it for deepening the Great Depression, when prices in the U.S. fell by roughly a quarter.

This is why central bankers, who spend most of their time fighting inflation, fear its opposite even more. A little inflation is a nuisance; deflation, once entrenched, is a trap that's historically hard to climb out of — just ask Japan about the 1990s.

Zoom out on any modern economy and you'll see a rhythm: a few good years - hiring sprees, rising wages, confident headlines - then a stumble, a slump, a slow climb back, and off we go again. Growth doesn't move in a straight line; it breathes.

That rhythm is the business cycle: the alternation between expansions, when the economy grows, and recessions, when it shrinks. "Cycle" oversells the regularity - expansions have run anywhere from a single year to over a decade, and the turns catch forecasters off guard with humbling consistency. But the pattern of boom and bust has held for as long as industrial economies have existed.

Explaining it is one of macroeconomics' great contests. Keynes pointed to swings in spending and mood - "animal spirits." Monetarists like Friedman blamed central banks mismanaging the money supply. Austrians blamed cheap credit inflating booms that must bust; Schumpeter saw waves of innovation churning the economy. Real-world cycles likely mix all of these - which is why the debate, like the cycle, keeps coming back around.

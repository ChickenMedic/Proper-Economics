---
term: Real vs. nominal
slug: real-vs-nominal
short: Nominal is the sticker number; real is what it buys after inflation.
related: [inflation, gdp]
---

You get a 3% raise — congratulations! Then you notice prices rose 5% that year. Your paycheck number went up, but it buys less than before. In the language of economics: your *nominal* wage rose, but your *real* wage fell.

Nominal means the raw number, in the money of the day. Real means the number after stripping out inflation — what the money actually buys. It sounds like bookkeeping, but confusing the two is one of the most common ways to be fooled by numbers. "Biggest box office of all time!" usually means ticket prices went up. "Record-high salaries" from decades ago can be quietly enormous once you translate them to today's dollars.

Economists deflate almost everything — wages, GDP, interest rates — into real terms before drawing conclusions. It's a good habit for reading the news, too. When a number spans years, your first question should be: is that real, or just nominal?

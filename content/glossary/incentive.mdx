---
term: Incentive
slug: incentive
short: A reward or penalty that changes what people choose to do.
related: [opportunity-cost, price-signal, nudge]
---

When a city starts charging for plastic bags - even a token amount - bag use tends to fall dramatically, sometimes by more than half. A few cents! Nobody's budget changed. But the nudge at the register changed the choice, checkout after checkout, millions of times.

An incentive is anything - a reward, a penalty, a price - that tilts people toward one choice over another. It's the closest thing economics has to a first principle: people respond to incentives, and most of the field is working out the consequences. Prices are incentives. Taxes and subsidies are incentives. So are grades, bonuses, speed cameras, and your gym's cancellation fee.

The fun (and the trouble) starts when incentives backfire. Colonial Hanoi once paid a bounty per rat tail; enterprising residents started farming rats. Pay surgeons per operation and you get more operations, needed or not. "What does this rule *actually* reward?" is the economist's reflex question - and once you acquire it, you'll never read a company policy the same way again.

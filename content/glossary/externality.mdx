---
term: Externality
slug: externality
short: A cost or benefit of a deal that lands on someone who wasn't part of it.
related: [public-good, commons, incentive]
---

Your neighbor's 2 a.m. drum practice costs you sleep, but he doesn't pay you a cent for it. A factory sells cheap paint and the river downstream picks up the tab. In each case, a deal between two parties dumps a cost on a third who never agreed to anything.

That spillover is an externality: a cost (or benefit) from an activity that lands on bystanders and never shows up in the price. Negative externalities - pollution is the classic - mean society gets too much of something, because the people choosing it aren't paying its full cost. Positive externalities run the other way: your flu shot protects your coworkers, your restored house lifts the whole street, so society tends to get too little of those.

Externalities are the textbook case of markets misfiring, and the classic fix, from the economist Arthur Pigou, is to build the missing cost into the price - a pollution tax, say. Ronald Coase added a twist: when the affected parties can negotiate cheaply, they can sometimes sort it out themselves. Carbon taxes, congestion charges, and cigarette taxes are all this one idea wearing different hats.

---
term: Marginal utility
slug: marginal-utility
short: The extra satisfaction you get from one more unit of something.
related: [marginal, subjective-value, demand]
---

The first glass of water after a long run is glorious. The second is nice. By the fifth, you're just being polite to your water bottle. Each glass is identical - but the *enjoyment* each one adds keeps shrinking.

That extra enjoyment from one more unit is marginal utility, and the shrinking is so reliable economists call it diminishing marginal utility. It solved a puzzle that stumped Adam Smith: why is water, which keeps you alive, nearly free, while diamonds, which do nothing much, cost a fortune? Because prices track the value of the *next* unit, not the total. Water is abundant, so your next glass matters little. Diamonds are scarce, so the next one still dazzles.

It also explains everyday behavior: why you don't spend your whole paycheck on your favorite food, why variety beats repetition, and why the tenth pair of shoes thrills less than the first. Value lives at the margin.

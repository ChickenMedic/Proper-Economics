---
term: Surplus value
slug: surplus-value
short: In Marx's theory, the value workers create beyond what they're paid.
related: [labor-theory-of-value, means-of-production, capital]
---

Say you work an eight-hour shift and, by Marx's arithmetic, the value you produce in the first five hours covers your whole day's wage. What about the other three hours? You're still producing - but that value goes to your employer. Those three hours are what Karl Marx called surplus value.

In Marx's system, surplus value is where profit comes from. Workers must sell their labor to live; employers own the workplaces (the means of production) and pay wages worth less than what the labor produces; the gap is surplus value, and collecting it, Marx argued, is exploitation built into the wage system itself - no villainous boss required. It's the load-bearing concept of *Capital*.

The idea stands or falls with the labor theory of value beneath it. Mainstream economists dropped that theory in the 1870s, explaining profit instead as the return for risk, waiting, and organizing production - so they don't use surplus value at all. But the intuition it captures, that workers may not get the full value of what they make, has never stopped fueling arguments.

term: Inflation
slug: inflation
short: A general rise in prices that shrinks what your money buys.

Ask your parents what a movie ticket cost when they were young, then brace for the story. A ticket that cost a couple of dollars decades ago runs ten times that now — and it's not because movies got ten times better. The dollar itself buys less than it used to.

That's inflation: a rise in prices across the whole economy, not just one product. One item getting pricier is just supply and demand at work. Inflation is when *almost everything* creeps up together, which is really another way of saying money is losing value.

A slow creep — around 2% a year, the target most central banks aim for — is considered normal, even helpful. The trouble starts when it speeds up: savings quietly melt, paychecks lag behind grocery bills, and planning ahead gets harder. Milton Friedman argued inflation is "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" — too much money chasing too few goods — though economists still debate how much else, from oil shocks to supply chains, shares the blame.

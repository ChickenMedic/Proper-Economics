---
term: Tariff
slug: tariff
short: A tax on goods coming into a country from abroad.
related: [free-trade, comparative-advantage, rent-seeking]
---

A tariff is a tax on imports. Slap a 25% tariff on foreign steel, and foreign steel gets 25% more expensive the moment it crosses the border. Domestic steelmakers cheer - their competition just got pricier. Everyone who *buys* steel, from carmakers to construction firms, quietly picks up the bill, and passes it along to you.

That's the recurring pattern with tariffs: visible winners, invisible payers. The protected industry and its jobs are easy to point to; the slightly higher price on thousands of products, spread across every household, is not. Frédéric Bastiat built a career skewering this in the 1840s - his satirical petition had French candlemakers demanding protection from a ruthless foreign rival: the sun.

Governments still use tariffs, sometimes to shield young industries or for national security, often because protected industries lobby hard. Economists across the spectrum are unusually united in their skepticism - tariffs tend to cost consumers more than they save in protected jobs - which hasn't stopped tariffs from being one of history's most popular policies.

## The idea in one paragraph

Open a textbook and markets teem with buyers and sellers, none big enough to matter. Now look out the window: one hospital hiring all the town's nurses, a handful of chains selling most of the groceries. Joan Robinson rebuilt economics for the world outside the window. Her *Economics of Imperfect Competition* (1933) gave us the tools for firms with real pricing power - and coined "monopsony," the market with one big *buyer*, which is why economists today can argue that a minimum wage might raise employment instead of killing it. She helped Keynes build his revolution, then spent decades asking a question the mainstream never quite answered: what do we even mean by "capital"?

## The world they lived in

Robinson was born Joan Maurice in Surrey in 1903, into a family with a taste for principled trouble - her father, a general, ended his career by publicly accusing the government of lying about the First World War. She read economics at Girton College, Cambridge, in the 1920s, when the subject was <E to="alfred-marshall">Alfred Marshall</E>'s kingdom and women could sit the exams but not take degrees. She married the economist Austin Robinson, spent two years in India, and returned in 1929 to a Cambridge - and a Britain - sliding into mass <G term="unemployment">unemployment</G> that Marshall's serene toolkit seemed unable even to describe.

Then came the most exciting seminar room of the century. In 1930–31 she joined "the Circus" - Richard Kahn, Piero Sraffa, James Meade, Austin - the small group whose weekly dissection of <E to="john-maynard-keynes">John Maynard Keynes</E>'s *Treatise on Money* helped him think his way toward the *General Theory*. Robinson became one of Keynesianism's fiercest expositors and, after the war, the leader of the "post-Keynesian" school that pushed his ideas into the long run. Cambridge made her wait until 1965 for a full professorship - a delay her admirers blame squarely on her sex. She died in 1983, having changed the subject twice and been denied its biggest prize.

## What they argued

Start in 1933, with a 29-year-old taking on the textbook. Marshall's world assumed "perfect competition": so many small firms that each just accepts the market price. Robinson pointed out that almost no real firm lives there. The village grocer, the branded soap maker, the local railway - each faces its own downward-sloping <G term="demand">demand</G> curve; raise your price and you lose *some* customers, not all of them. Her *Economics of Imperfect Competition* worked out, with new tools like the <G term="marginal">marginal</G> revenue curve, how such firms set prices and output. The result: less produced, at higher prices, than perfect competition would deliver. (Edward Chamberlin at Harvard published a similar theory that same year, but Robinson's toolkit is the one that stuck in the classroom.)

Her most consequential invention was one word. Everyone knew "monopoly," one seller. Robinson coined **monopsony**: one buyer. Think of a mining town with a single pit, or a rural county with one hospital. The employer doesn't take the market wage; it *sets* the wage, and profits by setting it below what workers' labor is actually worth. And here's the twist that still powers policy fights: in a monopsony, a legal minimum wage doesn't have to destroy jobs. Forcing the wage up toward the competitive level can pull *more* people into work, because the employer was deliberately understaffing to keep pay low. Ninety years later, when economists debate studies showing minimum-wage rises with little or no job loss, they are arguing on ground Robinson surveyed first.

After the Keynesian revolution, she pushed it beyond the slump. Keynes had answered the urgent question - why does an economy get stuck with idle workers and idle machines? - but said little about growth over decades. Robinson's *The Accumulation of Capital* (1956) took on exactly that: how investment, profits, and technical progress interact over the long haul, and how easily a growing economy can wander off any golden path. She also did something almost nobody at Cambridge would: she read <E to="karl-marx">Karl Marx</E> carefully and wrote about him fairly, treating him in her *Essay on Marxian Economics* (1942) as a flawed economist worth arguing with rather than a devil or a prophet.

Her last great fight was the Cambridge capital controversy - Cambridge, England versus Cambridge, Massachusetts, home of <E to="paul-samuelson">Paul Samuelson</E> and MIT. Mainstream growth models treated "<G term="capital">capital</G>" as a single measurable quantity, like tons of wheat, plugged into a production function. Robinson asked the awkward question: how do you add a crane, a sewing machine, and a server into one number? Only by using their prices - but their prices depend on profits and wages, which are what the model was supposed to explain. The reasoning is circular. Worse, she and her allies showed the "reswitching" paradox: the same technique can be most profitable at low *and* high interest rates, scrambling the tidy story that cheaper capital smoothly means more capital used. The honest scorecard: Samuelson conceded in a famous 1966 paper that the Cambridge, England side was logically right - and then the mainstream, finding the models too useful to abandon, largely shrugged and carried on. Robinson won the argument and lost the war.

## Where it breaks down

Her critics get their say. On imperfect competition, Robinson herself later disowned the book's static, geometry-heavy method as unrealistic - an author savaging her own classic. On the capital controversy, mainstream economists such as Robert Solow have argued that while the logical point stands, aggregate models remain useful approximations, and that the Cambridge school never built a workable replacement - a criticism even some sympathizers accept. Whether monopsony power is widespread enough to make minimum-wage rises harmless is still contested: the empirical literature finds small job losses in some studies and none in others.

The gravest charge is political, and it is fair. From the 1950s on, Robinson praised Mao's China - including sympathetic writing on the Cultural Revolution - and, after a 1964 visit, hailed North Korea's "miracle," suggesting Kim Il Sung's system would outshine the South. Events falsified this about as brutally as events can. Critics, including David Henderson in his Concise Encyclopedia of Economics profile, argue these views likely cost her the Nobel Prize many expected her to win in 1975. Her defenders call the late politics a tragic coda to first-rate science; nobody calls it defensible.

## Lasting influence

Robinson is routinely named the greatest economist never to win the Nobel - an omission that says more about the prize than about her. Her fingerprints are everywhere labor economics now works: the modern "new monopsony" literature, from Alan Manning's *Monopsony in Motion* to studies of hospital chains and warehouse towns, runs on her insight, and antitrust regulators have begun policing employers' power over workers, not just sellers' power over consumers.

The post-Keynesian school she led survives as the loyal opposition inside macroeconomics, insisting that history, uncertainty, and distribution belong in the models. And the capital controversy, though filed away, resurfaces whenever economists ask what national statistics on "capital" really measure. She liked to say the purpose of studying economics is to avoid being deceived by economists. It reads like a joke; she meant it as a syllabus.

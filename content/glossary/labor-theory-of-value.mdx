---
term: Labor theory of value
slug: labor-theory-of-value
short: The idea that a thing's value comes from the work put into it.
related: [surplus-value, subjective-value, capital]
---

A hand-carved chair takes forty hours to make; a plastic one pops out of a mold in seconds. It feels natural that the carved chair should be worth more - and that intuition, that the work poured into something is what gives it value, is the labor theory of value.

It wasn't a fringe idea. Adam Smith and David Ricardo both worked with versions of it, and Karl Marx built his whole system on it: if labor is the source of value, and workers get paid less than the value they create, then profit is essentially unpaid work - what Marx called surplus value. One theory of price, and suddenly you have a theory of exploitation.

The mainstream moved on in the 1870s, when the marginal revolution flipped the arrow: things aren't valuable because labor went into them; labor is worth paying for because people value what it makes. Spend forty hours carving a chair nobody wants and you've made an expensive mistake, not forty hours of value. Marxist economists have counterarguments, and the debate - though no longer central - never fully closed.

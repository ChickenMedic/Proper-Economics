## The big idea

Mercantilists believed a country's wealth was the pile of gold and silver in its vaults, and that the way to grow the pile was to sell more to foreigners than you bought from them. Exports good, imports bad, colonies useful, rivals dangerous. Trade, in this view, was a contest with winners and losers — and the government's job was to make sure your side won.

## When and why it rose

Mercantilism dominated European thinking from roughly the 1500s to the 1700s, and it made a certain rough sense at the time. Kings needed treasure to pay armies, and armies were busy: this was the age of near-constant war between Spain, France, England, and the Dutch. Gold *was* national power in a very literal way.

So governments acted accordingly. They slapped a <G term="tariff">tariff</G> on imports, subsidized exports, granted monopolies to trading companies like the East India Company, and ran colonies as captive customers — America could sell raw cotton to Britain but had to buy its finished cloth back. Writers like Thomas Mun in England argued the case openly: the goal of trade policy was a favorable balance, full stop.

## What it got right

More than its reputation suggests. Mercantilists grasped that trade, manufacturing, and state capacity were connected — that a country with shipyards, mills, and skilled workers was stronger than one without. Their obsession with building domestic industry looks a lot like the "infant industry" playbook that Japan, South Korea, and others later used with real success (though economists still argue about how much credit the playbook deserves — that one's contested). They also invented much of the machinery of economic statecraft: trade statistics, customs administration, national accounting in embryo. And they were right that money and power travel together, a point every sanctions regime since has confirmed.

## Where it fell short

The core error was treating wealth as gold rather than as the stuff gold can buy. <E to="adam-smith">Adam Smith</E> made this the opening target of *The Wealth of Nations* in 1776: a nation is rich when its people have more food, clothes, and houses — not when its king has more coins. David Hume had already shown the hoarding strategy defeats itself: gold flowing in pushes your prices up, making your exports expensive and imports cheap until the gold flows back out.

Worse, the win-lose picture of trade is simply wrong. Voluntary exchange benefits both sides — that's why both sides agree to it. And in practice, mercantilist privileges went to whoever lobbied hardest, an early masterclass in <G term="rent-seeking">rent-seeking</G>: monopolies and tariffs enriched connected merchants while everyone else paid more for worse goods.

## Its fingerprints today

Mercantilism never really died; it just changed clothes. Every time a politician calls a trade deficit "losing to China," promises to bring jobs "back home" with tariffs, or measures success by exports, you're hearing its echo. Modern industrial policy — chip subsidies, export controls, strategic stockpiles — is mercantilist reasoning with better spreadsheets, and the debate over whether it works is still live. Economists mostly treat "neo-mercantilism" as a warning label. But its central instinct, that trade is also about national power, is having an undeniable comeback.

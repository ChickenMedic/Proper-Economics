---
term: Central planning
slug: central-planning
short: Government officials, not markets, deciding what gets produced and at what price.
related: [price-signal, socialism, market-economy]
---

Imagine one office deciding, for an entire country, how much bread to bake, how much steel to forge, how many shoes to stitch - and in what sizes. No prices moving on their own, no businesses reading the room. Just targets, quotas, and reports flowing up and orders flowing down. That's central planning, and the Soviet Union's Gosplan agency ran a version of it for six decades.

The appeal is obvious: markets can look wasteful and chaotic, so why not coordinate everything rationally, on purpose? The catch, argued Ludwig von Mises and Friedrich Hayek, is that planners lose the very tool coordination depends on. Without real prices, there's no way to compare the value of options (should this steel become tractors or bridges?), and no way to gather the local knowledge scattered across millions of people. The result, historically: gluts of what nobody wanted and queues for what everyone did.

Planning isn't useless everywhere - wartime mobilization, with one clear goal, is its best showing. But as a way to run a whole peacetime economy, it kept failing in the same instructive way. You can feel the problem yourself in this site's price-signals interactive, where you get to be the planner.

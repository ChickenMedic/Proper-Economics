---
term: Market economy
slug: market-economy
short: An economy steered by prices and voluntary exchange, not government orders.
related: [central-planning, price-signal, invisible-hand]
---

Nobody is in charge of feeding your city. No ministry decides how many bagels it needs on a Tuesday. Yet the bagels show up - because bakers, truckers, and flour mills each responded to prices and made their own calls. An economy coordinated that way, by voluntary buying and selling rather than by orders from above, is a market economy.

Its defining features: private ownership, freedom to trade, and prices that float - rising when things get scarce, falling when they don't. Those moving prices do the coordinating, signaling what to make more of and what to quit making. The alternative pole is central planning, where officials set production and prices directly; the twentieth century ran that experiment at scale, and it didn't go well.

In practice, every real economy is mixed. Even the most market-friendly countries have public schools, safety rules, and central banks; the question is the blend, not the extremes. This site's perspective, stated openly: markets are a remarkably good default, and the interesting arguments are about where they need help.

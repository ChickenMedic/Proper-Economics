---
term: Free trade
slug: free-trade
short: Letting goods cross borders without taxes or barriers.
related: [tariff, comparative-advantage, laissez-faire]
---

Check the labels on the things around you right now: a phone from one country, a shirt from another, coffee from a third. You traded with half the planet before lunch, and everyone involved said yes voluntarily - that's rather the point.

Free trade means letting goods and services cross borders without special taxes (tariffs), quotas, or other barriers. The case for it starts with a simple observation: nobody thinks a city should grow all its own food, so why should a country make everything itself? David Ricardo sharpened this into comparative advantage - countries prosper by doing what costs them least and trading for the rest - and most economists have backed freer trade ever since.

The honest fine print: the gains are real but spread thin across millions of consumers, while the costs land hard on particular workers and towns when industries move abroad. Economists broadly agree trade makes nations richer overall; the fights - and they are perennial - are about who bears the cost of the adjustment, and what's owed to them.
